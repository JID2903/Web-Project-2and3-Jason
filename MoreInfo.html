Where was the Treaty Signed?

Treaty 4, also known as the Qu'Appelle Treaty, was signed between September 15 and 25, 1874, at Fort Qu'Appelle in what is now southern Saskatchewan. This agreement was made between representatives of the Canadian Crown and leaders of the Cree, Saulteaux (Anishinaabe), and Assiniboine (Nakota) nations. The treaty negotiations were influenced by several factors, including the decline of the buffalo population, food scarcity, and tensions arising from the Hudson's Bay Company's sale of Indigenous lands without consultation. The Indigenous communities sought to secure their rights and livelihoods amidst these challenges, leading to the establishment of Treaty 4. The treaty covered a vast area that now encompasses parts of southern Saskatchewan, western Manitoba, and southeastern Alberta. Following the initial signing, additional adhesions occurred at locations such as Fort Ellice, Swan Lake, Fort Pelly, and Fort Walsh, extending the treaty's reach and impact.

What are the Historic terms of the Treaty 4?

The historic terms of Treaty 4, signed in 1874, included several key provisions aimed at establishing a framework for the relationship between the Crown and the Indigenous nations involved. The treaty provided for the following:

  • Land Surrender: Indigenous nations agreed to surrender their rights to a vast area of land in exchange for certain benefits and protections.
  • Reserve Creation: The treaty established specific reserve lands for the Indigenous communities, where they could continue their traditional ways of life.
  • Annual Payments: The treaty included provisions for annual payments to the Indigenous signatories, providing them with financial support.
  • Education and Health Care: The treaty promised access to education and health care services for the Indigenous communities.
  • Hunting and Fishing Rights: Indigenous nations retained their rights to hunt, fish, and gather on the surrendered lands, ensuring their ability to maintain traditional practices.
